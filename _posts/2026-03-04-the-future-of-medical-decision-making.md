55M with uncontrolled diabetes mellitus and compensated cirrhosis comes to the hospital for ankle pain for the last month. He has been having fevers for the last week as well. The right ankle has been swollen and tender, but he is still able to bear weight on it. He is still able to walk up to 1 mile. He denies trauma, falls. He denies swelling in other joints or prior episodes. The pain is somewhat relieved with tylenol. The remaining review of systems is negative. He takes metformin for diabetes but no other medications. 
Vitals signs are notable for tachycardia
Examination of the joint reveals tenderness around the right ankle, swelling
pulses are palpable 

Does this patient need antibiotics immediately ?

This was a patient i saw in the last year of residency- many details have been modified to prevent indentification. the patient turned out to have septic arthritis- MSSA. He was taken emergently for joint washout and debridement after the arthrocentesis revealed wbc of 5000 and the gram stain came back with GPC- mSSA on culture. 

It goes without saying that this is atypical case. However medicine is often atypical and chaotic. For now, we have sophisticated sepsis prediction models. Large language models roughly simulate an intelligent physician. However this only brings us to the current standard of care, where the physician cannot say for sure what will happen but draws of knowledge of physiology to guide treatment. 

Consider the following questions in this case: 

what is the risk of septic arthritis in a subacute presentation ?
If the patient said that he suddenly developed pain, swelling in his right ankle one month ago how does that change things?
If the patient said the he gradually developed pain and swelling over the last 6 months and it had become unbearable in the last month , how does that change things ?
If the the patient had two swollen and red fingers in addition to the right ankle swelling, how does that change things?
If the patient had an ulcer underneath the foot in addition to the ankle swellilng how does that change things?
If the patient had severe pain in the foot, and an ulcer on the tip of the toe how does that change things ?

ALl this information is encoded in the real world- and often obscured in many patients. If the patient had been an 80 year old with dementia, with no recollection of the temporality of the symptoms. if the patient was hypotensive and not verbal 

So what is the future of medicine ? I saw this patient last year at the end of residency, and am beginning my career as an internist. What is the decision making tool of the future? I doubt current AI will really bring us to superintelligence. the academic centres of today effectively function as a kind of hive mind superintelligence of the present day. HOw can we encode this as an information technology - that can be endlessly replicated in any part of the world? So that anybody in any part of the world has access to explainable, grounded medical decision making?
